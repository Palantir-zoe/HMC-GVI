 知参数的后验分布通常没有明确的解析形式。实际中是用近似方法从数值上估计
 后验分布。两种常用的近似方法是马尔可夫链蒙特卡罗（Markov chain Monte Carlo,
 MCMC）和变分推断（variational inference, VI）。基于采样的 MCMC 方法能保证
 产生（渐进）精确的样本，但往往计算量很大。而基于优化的 VI 方法在计算速度
 和针对维度的扩展性方面有优势，但结果的准确性没有保证。本研究的目标是结
 合 MCMC 与 VI 两类方法，产生一类在性能上能同时兼具两者的新算法。
    本研究提出的第一个新算法是利用具有不同协方差结构的高斯变分推断
（Gaussian variational inference, GVI）来估计哈密顿蒙特卡罗（Hamiltonian Monte
 Carlo, HMC）算法中用于线性变换的预处理矩阵。该方法解决了在高维模型中计
 算预处理矩阵困难的问题。本文中对该方法的测试包括从维度和复杂性逐步增加
 的目标分布中进行采样。测试结果表明，使用 GVI 生成预处理矩阵的 HMC 在效
 率上优于标准的 MCMC 算法。随着目标分布的维度和复杂性增加，这种优势会变
 得更加明显。
    第二个新提出的算法是将基于 VI 的生成模型–变分自动编码器（variational
 auto-encoder, VAE）和 Metropolis–Hastings（MH）算法相结合，结合的具体方法是
 使用 VAE 生成 MH 的提议分布。这种创新的混合方法称为“MH-VAE”算法，与
 其他现有的 MCMC 算法相比，它易于执行并且能更有效地探索整个参数空间。使
 用峰值相隔较远的多维混合高斯分布和多维混合 t 分布对该方法进行测试的结果
 表明，MH-VAE 算法总是能够成功地在多峰分布的所有峰值处采样，而 MH 算法
 经常卡在局部峰值处，导致无法收敛。

                                    ABSTRACT

     Bayesian statistics makes inference based on Bayes’ theorem, and the biggest chal-
lenge faced by Bayesian inference is that the posterior distribution of the unknown param-
eters is typically analytically intractable. In practice, the posterior is estimated numeri-
cally using approximation methods, and two widely used such methods are Markov Chain
Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference (VI). Sampling-based MCMC methods
can guarantee to produce (asymptotically) exact samples, but tend to be computationally
intense, while optimizing-based VI methods are superior in speed and scalability but may
suffer from inaccuracy. The goal of this study is to combine the MCMC and VI methods
and produce a new class of algorithms that incorporate the strengths of both.
     The first newly proposed algorithm is to utilize Gaussian variational inference (GVI)
with various covariance structures to derive a preconditioning matrix for the linear trans-
formation in Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC). This method addresses the challenge
of computing preconditioning matrices for high-dimensional models. The tests of this
method include sampling from target distributions of increasing dimensions and com-
plexity. The results of the tests show that the HMC with preconditioning matrix produced
by GVI outperforms standard MCMC algorithms in eﬀiciency. This superiority becomes
more pronounced as the dimension and complexity of the target distribution increases.
     The second newly proposed algorithm is to combine a VI-based generative model,
variational auto-encoder (VAE), with the Metropolis–Hastings (MH) sampler, by using
VAE to produce the proposal distribution of MH. This innovative hybrid method, termed
the “VAE-MH” sampler, is straightforward to implement and more eﬀicient in travers-
ing the whole parameter space than state-of-the-art MCMC algorithms. The tests of this
method using both multi-dimensional mixture Gaussian and mixture t distributions with
distant modes show that the VAE-MH sampler can always successfully identify all the
modes of a multi-modal distribution while the MH algorithm often gets trapped in local
modes and fails to converge.


Keywords: Bayesian inference; Eﬀiciency; Gaussian Variational Inference; Markov
            Chain Monte Carlo; Variational Auto–Encoder

1.1 Motivation
     Bayesian statistics makes inference based on Bayes’ theorem. Specially, consider the
model 𝑝(x|𝜽), where x is the data, and 𝜽 is the unknown parameter in the model. Given
the prior distribution 𝑝(𝜽), by Bayes’s theorem, we obtain the posterior distribution of 𝜽,
                                𝑝(𝜽)𝑝(x|𝜽)        𝑝(𝜽)𝑝(x|𝜽)
                       𝑝(𝜽|x) =              =                    .
                                   𝑝(x)         ∫ 𝑝(𝜽)𝑝(x|𝜽)d𝜽
However the integral in the denominator is not feasible in practical scenarios. Since the
posterior distribution plays the central role in Bayesian analysis, approximating the pos-
terior without doing the integral is the main task of Bayesian inference. Markov Chain
Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference (VI) are two primary approaches to esti-
mating posterior distributions. Both approaches have their advantages and disadvantages.
     MCMC has been the gold standard for sampling from posterior distributions since
it can guarantee sampling from the exact target distribution. The Metropolis–Hastings
(MH) [1-2] is one of the most popular MCMC methods. MH generates samples from the
target distribution by proposing candidates from a proposal distribution and accepting
them with a probability. The random-walk MH algorithm is particularly attractive due to
its generality and ease of implementation. However, the proposal distribution of a random-
walk MH only generates local moves, which travel to the high-density region by chance. If
the dimension of the target distribution is high, the random-walk MH has low acceptance
rate, highly correlated samples and its eﬀiciency decreases quickly [3] . In addition, a well-
known challenge for standard MCMC methods is their poor mixing when dealing with
multi-modal target distributions with distant modes. Many MCMC algorithms struggle to
jump over the low-probability region between modes, and may get stuck at one mode.
     VI is an optimization-based approach to approximating distributions [4-6] . In contrast
to sampling-based MCMC approaches, VI searches for the optimal distribution that min-
imizes the distance (typically measured by the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence) to the
target posterior from a set of tractable distributions. Compared to MCMC, VI is much
less expensive in computation and much more scalable with dimension [7] . However, VI
can introduce significant bias if all the distributions in the candidate family are far away
from the target distribution. Thus, the effect of VI highly depends on the choice of the

                                              1

candidate distributions [8] .
     As stated above, VI and MCMC methods have complementary strengths and weak-
nesses. Therefore, it is natural to expect that new algorithms with performance better than
both can be obtained by combining VI and MCMC. However, simply using the VI ap-
proximation as the proposal distribution of an MH sampler lead to algorithms with poor
scalability and mixing eﬀiciency [9-11] . In this study, we present two novel approaches to
combining VI and MCMC, which can truly utilize the strengths of both to generate more
eﬀicient algorithms.


1.2 Contribution
     The goal of this study is to combine the MCMC with VI methods and produces a
class of algorithms that incorporate the strengths of both.
     The first newly proposed algorithm is to utilize Gaussian variational inference (GVI)
with various covariance structures to derive a preconditioning matrix for the linear trans-
formation in HMC (Hamiltonian Monte Carlo). Specifically, we consider GVI with
Cholesky decomposed covariance [12] , GVI with factor decomposed covariance [13] and
GVI with sparse precision matrices [14] . This method addresses the challenge of comput-
ing preconditioning matrices for high-dimensional models [3] . The tests of this method in
this thesis include sampling from target distributions of increasing dimensions and com-
plexity. The results of the tests show that the HMC with preconditioning matrix produced
by GVI outperforms standard MCMC algorithms in eﬀiciency. This superiority becomes
more pronounced as the dimension and complexity of the target distribution increases.
     The second newly proposed algorithm is to combine VI-based generative model,
variational auto-encoder (VAE) [15] , with MH [2] sampler, by using VAE to produce the
proposal distribution of MH. This innovative hybrid method, termed the “VAE-MH” sam-
pler, is straightforward to implement and more eﬀicient in traversing the whole parameter
space than state-of-the-art MCMC algorithms. The tests of the method using both multi-
dimensional mixture Gaussian and mixture 𝑡 distributions with distant modes show that
the VAE-MH sampler can always successfully identify all the modes of a multi-modal dis-
tribution while the MH algorithm often gets trapped in local modes and fails to converge.

2.1.2    Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithms

     As stated in Chapter 1, MH is one of the most widely used MCMC methods. The
Metropolis algorithm (1953) [1,17] was first proposed, and Hasting et al. (1970) [2] gener-
alized the Metropolis algorithm by allowing the asymmetric in the proposal distribution.
The Gibbs sampler [18] is another widely used MCMC algorithms. It samples from the tar-
get distribution by drawing from the conditional distribution of each component variable.
Each step of a Gibbs sampler can be considered a special MH algorithm with acceptance
probability 1. While the Gibbs sampler is easy to implement when sampling from a multi-
dimensional distribution, it has been criticized for slow convergence. In this thesis, we
focus on the MH-type MCMC algorithms.
     A common choice for the proposal distribution in MH is a Gaussian or uniform dis-
tribution around the current state of the Markov chain. The corresponding MH algorithm
is also called random walk. The random-walk MH algorithm is easy to implement, but
does not have good mixing properties or scale well with dimension, because the moves it
proposes are local and random.
     Many studies for improving the eﬀiciency of the MH algorithm have been concen-
trated on choosing better proposal distributions. Yang et al. (2013) [19] introduced a novel
class of Bactrian kernels, which are able to propose samples distant from the current state.
Based on Yang et al. (2013), Thawornwattana et al. (2018) [20] introduced the “mirror”
proposal which can lead to Markov chains with negative autocorrelations and super eﬀi-
ciency. Some MH algorithms incorporate the gradient information of the target distribu-
tion in the proposal. Both the Metropolis adjusted Langevin algorithm (MALA) [3,21] and
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) [22] are such MH methods. MALA derives the proposal
from a discretized Langevin diffusion and HMC uses discretized Hamiltonian dynamics to
propose new values. Both methods are more eﬀicient and scalable than random-walk MH.
However, tuning the parameters of these methods is a challenge. To address this problem,
Girolami et al. (2011) [3] introduced Riemann-manifold MALA and HMC, which designs
MCMC algorithms on the Riemann manifold defined by the target distribution to account
for its geometric structure. However, Riemann-manifold MCMC algorithm are expensive
in computation. Hoffman et al. (2014) [23] proposed the No-U-Turn samplers (NUTS), an

                                             5
                                CHAPTER 2         PRELIMINARIES

extension of HMC that eliminates the necessity of manually tuning the algorithm.
     In addition, some adaptive MCMC algorithms are invented. For example, Haario et
al. (2001) [24] introduced the adaptive Metropolis (AM) algorithm, which automatically
adjusts the covariance matrix of proposal distribution to achieve a desired acceptance rate
of the MH algorithm.
     Multi-modal target distributions impose big challenges on the eﬀiciency of the stan-
dard MCMC algorithms since the latter typically have trouble jumping over low-density
areas between modes and will get stuck. Several strategies have been proposed to address
this challenge. One widely used technique is tempering, which heated the multi-modal
target distribution to flatten the modes. Specifically, the target distribution is raised by a
power 𝛽 ∈ (0, 1) and samples from the flattened distribution 𝑝(𝜃)𝛽 are facilitated to help
traverse between modes when sampling from the un-flattened target. Examples include
parallel tempering [25] and simulated tempering [26] . Other approaches for dealing with
multi-modal targets include designing special proposal distributions tailored to facilitate
movement between modes [27] .


2.2 Variational Inference
2.2.1    A brief introduction to variational inference

     Generative models are designed to learn the unknown data distribution using the data
from that distribution and generate new data that closely resemble the real data. Mathe-

     Popular generative models are Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), Variational
Auto-encoder (VAE), Normalizing flow and diffusion models. See figure 2-2, GAN [33]
consists of two parts, a generator and a discriminator. Both parts can be neural networks.
These two parts participate in a competitive game where the generator generates synthetic
data to fool the discriminator, whose task is then to distinguish real and synthetic data.
The generator and discriminator are trained simultaneously. If the global equilibrium is
reached, the distribution of both the generator and the discriminator are equal to the true
data distribution. Normalizing flow [34] is a generative model designed to convert a simple

or very close to the data distribution. This is achieved by a sequence of invertible and
differentiable transformations. Inspired by non-equilibrium thermodynamics, diffusion
models [35-36] first use a forward process to add noise to the data until the data becomes pure
noise. Then diffusion models learn the reverse of the forward process and use the reverse
process to recover the data distribution. VAE [15] assumes that the data are generated by

related to VI, it is the generative model that we consider when designing new algorithms.
A detailed introduction of VAE is in section 3.4.1.


2.4 Related Work
2.4.1    MCMC and VI: bridging the gap

     A logical inquiry emerges: is it feasible to merge MCMC and VI to alleviate their
individual shortcomings and harness the strengths of both? Some attempts have already
been made in this direction. De Freitas et al. (2001) [11] introduced an approach which
constructs a new blocked MH algorithm using the variational approximation as the pro-
posal distribution, and switches between the new MH and a local MH algorithm. This
                                              12
                              CHAPTER 2      PRELIMINARIES

strategy allows the quick identification of high-density regions, and at the same time avoids
getting stuck. Salimans et al. (2015) [37] introduced another approach that combines VI
and MCMC. They used a few samples of MCMC as auxiliary variables in the variational
approximation distribution to increase the flexibility of the variational distribution family.
Another approach incorporating auxiliary variables, called auxiliary variational MCMC,
was proposed by Habib et al. (2018) [38] . This method introduces auxiliary variables to
construct a joint distribution with the target distribution as the marginal. VI is then per-
formed to approximate this joint distribution and finally a new MCMC algorithm sampling
the joint distribution is constructed using the variational approximation as the proposal.
This new MCMC is able to avoid the random-walk behavior often encountered in tradi-
tional MCMC methods and enables more eﬀicient sampling.

2.4.2    Using generative models to assist MCMC algorithms

     The research in this direction so far has mostly been using the generative model to
construct the proposal distribution of the MH algorithm. Gabrie et al. (2021) [39] used
the MALA algorithm to train the normalizing flow and used the trained normalizing flow
to construct the proposal of a new MH algorithm. Hunt-Smith et al. (2024) [40] used the
random-walk MH to train the diffusion model and then the diffusion model is used to
construct the proposal distribution for a new MH algorithm. The new MH algorithms of
both Gabrie et al. (2021) and Hunt-Smith et al. (2024) are eﬀicient in sampling multi-
modal distributions.

3.2 Estimation of Covariance Matrices
3.2.1     Estimate covariance matrix with burn-in sample

     Sample covariance matrix obtained from the burn-in stage of the MCMC algorithm
is the most straightforward estimate of the target covariance matrix, and the estimate can
be accurate if the dimension of the target distribution is not too high. We typically run a
chain of 106 plus a burn-in of 104 iterations in our numerical studies [20] . The motivation
of using burn-in is to make the chain approximately converge at the end of this phase.
In fact, if the chain is far from convergence at the end of burn-in, the distribution of the
subsequent samples may be more biased than expected.

3.2.2     Gaussian variational inference

     Among the different VI approaches, Gaussian variational inference (GVI) is most
widely used. The GVI tries to find the distribution that closest to the target posterior from
a Gaussian distribution family. In this section, we will explore several variants of GVI and
introduce the strength and applications of each.

3.4.2    A VAE-assisted MCMC sampler

     Standard MCMC methods using local proposal distributions, which are centered
around the current state, mix poorly when sampling from the multi-modal target distri-
butions with distant modes, since these methods can hardly jump over the low-density
region between distant modes and typically end up getting stuck in one mode. There-
fore, novel MCMC algorithms which can eﬀiciently jump between modes are required for
sampling the multi-modal distributions.
     Inspired by Gabie et al. (2021) and Hunt-Smith et al. (2024) [39-40] , here we propose
a novel MCMC sampler called “VAE-MH”, which combines the Gaussian random-walk
MH algorithm with the VAE model. Specifically, we first train the VAE model to produce
a new proposal distribution, use this proposal to produce a new MH algorithm, and switch
between the new algorithm and the original random-walk MH within the whole algorithm.
Note that in this process we constantly re-train the VAE model to update the new proposal
so that the corresponding MH algorithm becomes better and better in performance. The
primary advantage of this new MCMC sampler is the global nature of its proposed values
so that it is not easy for the algorithm to get stuck in a local area. We now give a detailed
description of how to combine the VAE model with the random-walk MH to produce the
new MH algorithm.

𝑄(𝜃1 ). We continue this process until we obtain the last conditional distribution 𝑄(𝜃𝑑 ∣
𝜃1 , 𝜃2 , 𝜃3 , … , 𝜃𝑑−1 ).
      This approach was primarily chosen to reduce computational cost and circumvent
the necessity for constructing high-dimensional histograms. The number of bins, 𝑏, is
calculated using the Sturges’ Rule, that is,

                                        𝑏 = 1 + log2 𝐾,

which is a simple method for determining the number of bins based on the number of
available data points.
      The estimated 𝑄(𝜽) is a new proposal distribution. A new value 𝜽′ is generated by
randomly selecting one sample from {𝜽(𝑘) }𝐾
                                          𝑘=1 , which is used to estimate the proposal
distribution. Note that 𝜽′ generated this way does not depend on the current state of the
algorithm, 𝜽. Thus the new proposal 𝑄(𝜽) is an independent one. By using 𝑄(𝜽) as the
proposal distribution, we obtain a new independent MH algorithm.
      In each iteration of the whole algorithm, we use a certain probability denoted by 𝑝VAE
to decide if we implement the new independent MH or the original random-walk MH. At
this stage, we tuning 𝑝VAE manually. Note that we retrain the VAE model every 𝜏 iterations
using the last 4000 samples accumulated by then together with the initial samples used to
train the VAE model. Since the distribution of the training samples gets closer and closer

to the true target distribution, the proposal distribution constructed by the VAE model will
be a more accurate approximation of the target distribution and generate global proposals
which can easily jump to any region with high density in the parameter space.
     The procedure of the complete new VAE-MH algorithm is described in Algorithm 3.5
below. In our experiments of Section 4.3, when training the VAE model, we use the default

number of 𝝐 samples to estimate the ELBO to be 𝐿 = 1. For the MCMC algorithm, we

                                  CONCLUSION

     1. Main Results
     The goal of this study is to combine the MCMC algorithms with VI methods to
produce a novel class of algorithms that incorporate the strengths of both. We propose
two methods to combine MCMC and VI. The first is to use GVI to estimate the covariance
matrix deployed by the HMC for linear transformation, and the second is to produce a new
MH algorithm of which the proposal is produced by the VI-based generative model, VAE.
By using the numerical studies to illustrate the effects of these two methods, we get the
following three main conclusions.
     （1） We find that moment estimates obtained from the burn-in stage of an MCMC
algorithm which runs approximately the same amount of time as GVI are much less accu-
rate. This is because the Markov chain is far from convergence at the end of burn-in, the
distribution of the these samples is biased and the problem becomes more severe as the
dimension increases. GVI is much more computationally eﬀicient in calculating the mo-
ment estimates of a distribution compared to MCMC and its estimation accuracy remains
stable with the dimension of the target distribution increasing.
     （2） Based on the experiment results of sampling from the logistic regression pos-
terior distribution for the 8-dimensional Pima Indian dataset, the 24-dimensional German
credit dataset, the 100-dimensional Gaussian target and 509-dimensional GLMM poste-
rior distribution, distinct trends are observed in the comparison between GVI and MCMC
in terms of the computational eﬀiciency and the accuracy of estimating the covariance ma-
trix. In low-dimension cases, the GVI and MCMC burn-in of 104 iterations take around
the same amount of time. But GVI provides a more accurate covariance matrix estimate
than MCMC burn-in, resulting in enhanced eﬀiciency (𝐸) of the MCMC algorithms for
the transformed variables. In high-dimensional settings, GVI takes significantly shorter
time than MCMC burn-in, while still provides a more accurate covariance matrix esti-
mate. Thus estimating the covariance matrix with GVI leads to higher eﬀiciency (𝐸) of
the HMC algorithm with linear transformation and shorter total sampling time. In all,
GVI emerges as a promising alternative for the MCMC burn-in when estimating the co-
variance matrix, since it provides superior eﬀiciency and accuracy, and its effects are
robust for high-dimensional and complex target distributions.

                                            52
                                        CONCLUSION

     （3） The new VAE-MH sampler that we proposed leverages the VAE model to
capture the low-dimensional latent structures of the parameter space and generates a pro-
posal distribution with global nature. Our experiments demonstrate that the new “VAE-
MH” sampler performs better much than state-of-the-art MCMC algorithms for sampling
multi-modal target distributions in terms of both eﬀiciency and computational time.
     2. Innovations
     There are two main innovative points in this study.
     （1） We innovatively propose to utilize GVI to derive a covariance matrix for the
linear transformation in HMC. What is more, we use GVI with various covariance struc-
tures. Specifically, we consider GVI with Cholesky decomposed covariance [12] , GVI
with factor decomposed covariance [13] and GVI with sparse precision matrices [14] . These
methods address the challenge of estimating covariance matrices for complex and high-
dimensional distributions.
     （2） We propose to combine a VI-based generative model, VAE, with the random-
walk MH sampler, by using the samples generated with the random-walk MH to train
VAE and then constructing a new independent MH algorithm with the proposal produced
by VAE. This innovative “VAE-MH” sampler is straightforward to implement and more
eﬀicient in traversing the whole parameter space than state-of-the-art MCMC algorithms.
     3. Prospects
     There are two directions that we can work on in the future.
     （1） We have used some examples in our study to demonstrate the superiority of
GVI over MCMC methods in estimating the covariance matrix and the eﬀiciency of VAE-
MH in sampling complex distributions compared to state-of-the-art MCMC algorithms.
However, further investigation is necessary to fully explore their capabilities. In the future,
we aim to assess their effectiveness of statistical inference in real application areas, such
as finance, biology, etc.
     （2） The VAE-MH sampler is a combination of one type of the generative models
and one basic MCMC algorithm. In the future, we will investigate the possibility and
effects of combining all sorts of generative models with more advanced MCMC algo-
rithms, including the reversible jump MCMC [53] , which is a generalization of MH and
allows jumping among parameter spaces with varying dimensions.
